# Job-Role-Profile Structure Specification

**Version:** 0.2.0
**Purpose:** Defines the canonical structure of a job-role-profile `.md` file — the complete assessment package for a specific role
**Used by:** Job-role-profile authoring (Claude Chat session using this spec; a UI wrapper is in backlog), Tier 1 and Tier 2 generation prompts, app runtime
**Upstream authority:** AI Readiness Literacy Constitution v0.2.0
**Companion artifacts:** `tier1-question-generation-prompt.md`, `tier2-question-generation-prompt.md`

---

## Overview

A job-role-profile is a single `.md` file that contains everything the assessment app needs to assess a person in a specific role. It has three parts:

1. **YAML front matter** — identifying metadata consumed by the app at runtime
2. **Role Context** (Sections 1–8) — authored in a Claude Chat session using this spec as the guide, describes the role and its world
3. **Assessment Questions** (Sections 9–10) — generated by the Tier 1 and Tier 2 generation prompts, reviewed and edited by the author, stored in the same file

The file is named `job-role-profile-[identifier].md` (e.g., `job-role-profile-general.md`, `job-role-profile-medical-billing.md`, `job-role-profile-legal-paralegal.md`).

---

## YAML Front Matter

Every job-role-profile begins with a YAML front matter block delimited by `---`. The app reads these fields at runtime and uses them across the welcome screen, persistent header, and PDF.

```yaml
---
role_identifier: "medical-billing"
role_label: "Medical Billing Specialist"
role_description: "An insurance billing clerk at a 10-physician multi-specialty medical practice. The role handles claim submission, denial management, prior authorizations, coordination of benefits, and patient billing inquiries — with AI tools increasingly part of the workflow."
version: "0.4.0"
---
```

**Field definitions:**

- **`role_identifier`** — short, hyphenated, lowercase string used as the URL slug (e.g., `wkpath.com/medical-billing`) and as the internal ID in `work-contexts.ts`. Must match the suffix of the filename after `job-role-profile-`. No spaces, no special characters, no quotes within the value.

- **`role_label`** — the display title shown in the welcome screen dropdown, the persistent header subtitle, and the PDF report header. Written as a human-readable title in title case (e.g., "Medical Billing Specialist," "Everyday Knowledge Work," "General Knowledge Work"). This is the primary way the role presents itself to the respondent.

- **`role_description`** — a short paragraph (2-4 sentences) shown as clickable expandable text behind the header subtitle link. This is the respondent's first orientation to what the assessment is about and who it is for. Should be concrete and specific: name the role or role type, establish the stakes or work context, and hint at where AI shows up. Avoid generic framing like "professionals who use AI in their work."

- **`version`** — semantic version of the profile itself, starting at `0.1.0` for new profiles. Version bumps follow the conventions in the "Versioning" section at the bottom of this spec.

**Why each matters:**

The `role_identifier` is the only one the app *must* have to function — it's the key everything else is looked up from. `role_label` and `role_description` shape respondent experience; if either is weak or stale, the whole assessment feels generic. `version` is what makes calibration reproducible — without it, we can't tell whether a profile run today used the same rubrics as the run last month.

## Part 1: Role Context

### Section 1: Organization Overview

**What it captures:** The type of organization, its size, industry, and operating environment. Broad context that frames everything else.

**What the author asks (in session):** "Tell me about the organization. What kind of work does it do? How big is it? What's the general working environment like?"

**What good looks like:** 3-5 sentences. Establishes industry, scale, and the nature of the work product (documents vs. physical goods vs. patient care vs. legal filings, etc.). Doesn't need to be a specific real company — a representative archetype works.

**Example signal words:** "professional services," "200 employees," "healthcare practice," "law firm," "manufacturing facility," "school district"

---

### Section 2: The Role

**What it captures:** Who this person is — their title or role type, what they do at a high level, where they sit in the organization, how much autonomy they have, and who they interact with.

**What the author asks (in session):** "Describe the role we're assessing. What's the title or type of position? What does this person do at a high level? How much autonomy do they have? Who do they work with?"

**What good looks like:** 3-5 sentences. Establishes the role's level, scope, and relationships. Should make clear whether this is an individual contributor, a coordinator, a specialist, a manager, etc. Autonomy level matters — it calibrates what decisions the person is expected to make independently.

**Why it matters for assessment:** The role description calibrates the entire assessment. A question that's appropriate for someone with high autonomy may be unrealistic for someone who escalates most decisions. The generation prompts use this to set scenario complexity.

---

### Section 3: AI Tools Currently in Use

**What it captures:** What AI tools the organization has made available, how they're being used, and what policies (formal or informal) exist around AI use.

**What the author asks (in session):** "What AI tools does this person have access to? How are people using them today? Are there any policies or guidelines about AI use?"

**What good looks like:** 4-6 bullet points or a short paragraph. Should distinguish between general-purpose AI assistants, AI features embedded in existing tools, and any domain-specific AI applications. Policy status matters — "no formal policy" is useful information.

**Why it matters for assessment:** The tools in use determine what scenarios feel realistic. If the organization uses AI-suggested billing codes, that's a scenario the person should encounter. If AI is only used for drafting emails, scenarios about automated workflows would feel foreign.

---

### Section 4: Common Tasks

**What it captures:** What a typical week looks like for this person. The actual work they do, not aspirational descriptions.

**What the author asks (in session):** "Walk me through a typical week for this person. What tasks take up their time? What do they produce? What are their recurring responsibilities?"

**What good looks like:** 6-10 bullet points. Concrete, specific tasks — not job description language. "Drafting appeal letters for denied claims" rather than "manages correspondence." Should cover the full range of the person's work, including both routine tasks and occasional higher-judgment work.

**Why it matters for assessment:** This is the scenario source material. Every Tier 1 and Tier 2 question should map to something in this list. If a task isn't here, a scenario about it will feel inauthentic.

---

### Section 5: Decision Authority and Accountability

**What it captures:** What decisions this person makes, at what level of autonomy, and what happens when those decisions are wrong. This is the section that differentiates roles within the same organization.

**What the author asks (in session):** "What decisions does this person make on their own? What decisions do they influence but escalate to someone else? When this person gets something wrong, what are the consequences — for them, for the organization, for the people they serve?"

**What good looks like:** Three bands of decision-making, described concretely:

- **Routine decisions they own outright** — high-volume, low-review, the person's individual judgment. What does the person decide without asking anyone? What goes out the door with only their eyes on it?
- **Judgment-embedded decisions that carry weight** — decisions that shape outcomes for others. Where does this person's judgment affect clients, patients, students, colleagues, or organizational direction? What happens when these go wrong?
- **Decisions they escalate but shape** — issues they bring to someone else, but where how they frame it matters. What does the person flag, and how does their framing influence the outcome?

Closing with a characterization of the error pattern: do mistakes accumulate slowly (erosion) or produce discrete traceable events (triggers)?

**Why it matters for assessment:** This is the primary lever for calibrating question difficulty and rubric specificity. The three bands map to tier difficulty: Tier 1 can target routine and judgment-embedded decisions, Tier 2 can push into judgment-embedded and escalation territory, Tier 3 can create pressure at the boundaries. The consequence characterization shapes the Judgment construct rubrics — a rubric for a role where errors trigger audits reads very differently from one where errors erode trust gradually.

---

### Section 6: What's High-Stakes Here

**What it captures:** The organizational and environmental stakes — what's at risk when things go wrong in this workplace, regardless of the specific role. Compliance requirements, reputational concerns, client/patient/student impact, financial exposure.

**What the author asks (in session):** "What are the biggest risks in this environment when it comes to AI use? What kinds of errors would matter most? Are there compliance, legal, or regulatory concerns?"

**What good looks like:** 4-6 bullet points describing concrete consequences. Should cover accuracy risks, confidentiality risks, reputational risks, and any regulatory or compliance dimensions. The closing should characterize the overall risk environment — regulated vs. unregulated, high-consequence vs. accumulative.

**How it relates to Section 5:** Section 5 describes what *this role* is accountable for. Section 6 describes what *the organization* has at stake. They reinforce each other. Section 5 says "this person submits claims." Section 6 says "incorrect claims can trigger fraud investigations." Together they create the full picture the question generator needs.

---

### Section 7: Language and Terminology

**What it captures:** The everyday vocabulary of this role and organization. Terms the person uses and hears daily.

**What the author asks (in session):** "What jargon or specialized terms does this person encounter every day? What would a new hire need to learn to understand conversations in this workplace?"

**What good looks like:** A comma-separated list of 15-25 terms. Should include role-specific terms, organization-specific terms, industry terms, and tool names. These get woven into scenarios to make them feel authentic.

**Why it matters for assessment:** Scenarios that use the right vocabulary feel real. Scenarios that use generic language feel like a quiz written by someone outside the field.

---

### Section 8: What We Want to Know

**What it captures:** The assessment question distilled into plain language. What does the person commissioning this assessment actually want to learn about the people taking it?

**What the author asks (in session):** "When someone finishes this assessment, what do you want to know about them? What's the question you're trying to answer?"

**What good looks like:** 2-4 sentences. Should be concrete and specific to the role. Not "are they AI literate" but "can this person use AI to process claims faster without introducing coding errors that affect compliance?"

**Why it matters for assessment:** This section is the North Star for the generation prompts. The questions they produce should collectively answer what's stated here. It's also the anchor for the profile generation prompt — the final profile should address what the commissioner wanted to know.

---

## Part 2: Assessment Questions

### Section 9: Tier 1 Questions

**What it contains:** The complete Tier 1 question set — 5 scenario-based questions with full 3×3 rubrics, metadata, and scoring instructions. Generated by `tier1-question-generation-prompt.md`, reviewed and edited by the author.

**Format:** JSON block embedded in the markdown file (fenced code block). Follows the output schema defined in the Tier 1 generation prompt.

**Relationship to Part 1:** Every scenario should trace back to the role context in Sections 1–8. The generation prompt uses all eight context sections as input.

---

### Section 10: Tier 2 Questions

**What it contains:** The complete Tier 2 question set — 5 scenario-based questions with full 3×3 rubrics, metadata, and scoring instructions. Generated by `tier2-question-generation-prompt.md` with the finalized Tier 1 questions as additional input. Reviewed and edited by the author.

**Format:** JSON block embedded in the markdown file (fenced code block). Follows the output schema defined in the Tier 2 generation prompt.

**Relationship to Tier 1:** Tier 2 questions complement Tier 1 — different angles, different DOL emphasis, different human functions activated. The Tier 2 generation prompt enforces this by requiring Tier 1 questions as input.

## Authoring Flow

Job-role-profiles are authored interactively in Claude Chat sessions using this spec as the guide. A UI wrapper (originally envisioned as a React interview tool) is in backlog but not built — current evidence suggests interactive chat authoring produces better profiles at this nascent stage, since we're still refining the spec itself and a UI over top of a shifting spec would need constant rework.

**The interview stages map to the eight context sections:**

| Stage | Section | Interview approach |
|-------|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Organization Overview | Open conversation — "Tell me about the organization" |
| 2 | The Role | Focused — "Describe the specific role" |
| 3 | AI Tools in Use | Inventory — "What AI tools are available?" |
| 4 | Common Tasks | Elicitation — "Walk me through a typical week" |
| 5 | Decision Authority | Structured — three bands of decision-making |
| 6 | High Stakes | Risk inventory — "What goes wrong and what matters?" |
| 7 | Terminology | Quick extraction — "What jargon does this person use?" |
| 8 | What We Want to Know | Synthesis — "What's the question you're trying to answer?" |

**End-to-end authoring sequence:**

1. Interactive session with the author covers the eight context sections
2. The eight sections are assembled into the job-role-profile format
3. Author reviews the assembled context, revises as needed
4. Tier 1 questions generated using the Tier 1 generation prompt with the profile as input
5. Author reviews Tier 1 questions, edits or regenerates
6. Tier 2 questions generated using the Tier 2 generation prompt with the profile + finalized Tier 1 as input
7. Author reviews Tier 2 questions, edits or regenerates
8. Complete `.md` file exported with YAML front matter + context + questions

**Optional companion artifact — calibration persona:** Every production-bound profile benefits from a companion persona file (see `persona-marcus-delgado.md`, `persona-jasmine-okafor.md` for the pattern). The persona is a fictional mid-Developing respondent with 10 canned responses and predicted scores, used to validate that the profile's scoring engine produces the expected narrative. Not every profile gets a persona immediately — they take real effort to author — but any profile headed toward a real deployment or calibration cycle should have one. File this with the sibling profiles in the repo.

## Stylistic Alignment

When authoring role_description, role_label, or any user-facing text within a profile, follow the language conventions in `CLAUDE.md`:

- Product name: "The WorkPath Assessment" (always use "The" with the full name in user-facing prose)
- Scoring dimensions: "Orientation," "Integration," "Judgment" (never "constructs" in user-facing text)
- Level names: "Emerging," "Developing," "Demonstrating"
- Grammatical preferences: "differently from" not "differently than"; "reveals how they think" not "listens for how they think"

These conventions don't appear in question text (which is role-specific and doesn't usually need to reference dimensions or levels), but they do appear in role_description, in any profile-level copy, and in any documentation that accompanies the profile.

### Profile files (job-role-profile-*.md)

Each job-role-profile carries its own `version` field in front matter. Version bumps follow semantic-versioning conventions adapted for this domain:

**Minor bump (0.X.0):** Substantive changes to the profile's assessment content. Triggers include:
- Question scenarios rewritten
- Rubric descriptors substantively changed across multiple questions
- New sections added to Role Context or structural changes to existing sections
- Calibration work that changes how responses will be scored

Example: general 0.3.0 → 0.4.0 when the mechanism-bar retune was applied to two Orientation rubrics.

**Patch bump (0.X.Y):** Targeted or minor updates that don't materially change the assessment as a whole. Triggers include:
- Single-question rubric retune
- Scenario text corrected or clarified without changing what the question measures
- Role_description, role_label, or other front-matter text refined
- Typo fixes, formatting, non-substantive copy edits

Example: medical-billing 0.4.0 → 0.4.1 when a single Orientation rubric was retuned without touching the rest of the profile.

**Starting version:** New profiles start at 0.1.0.

**Where to update:** The `version` field in YAML front matter AND the `meta.version` field inside both the Tier 1 and Tier 2 JSON blocks. Three locations per file. All three must move together or the profile is in an inconsistent state.
