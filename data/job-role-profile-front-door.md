# Job-Role-Profile: Everyday Knowledge Work

## Work Context

You work somewhere that expects mid-career professional judgment. The specifics of the workplace vary — a hospital, a school district, a regional bank, a municipal office, a mid-size manufacturer, a small business, a professional services firm, a nonprofit, a clinic. What's shared across these settings is the shape of the work: part of the day is with people or on your feet, and part is at a screen. The person doing this work is expected to own outcomes in their piece of the organization, coordinate with others, produce things that other people rely on, and make judgment calls about how to spend their time.

Technology in these workplaces is uneven. Some have modern tools and clear policies. Some are running on email, spreadsheets, and whatever software the business bought five years ago. AI tools are newly present in most of them — through general-purpose assistants people use directly, through features added to the software they already have, or through both — but usage is largely individual rather than coordinated.

## The Role

Mid-career professional with real responsibility and real autonomy. This person is not an entry-level employee learning the ropes, and they are not an executive setting strategy. They are the layer of the workforce that actually gets work done: managers of small teams, supervisors, coordinators, experienced individual contributors, owner-operators, practitioners who also handle administrative work. They have a degree or some post-secondary credential behind them — bachelor's, associate's, trade credential, bootcamp, significant experience — but what matters here is the position, not the diploma.

Their job splits across three kinds of work in roughly balanced proportion: physical and social work (being with people, moving through the day, handling what comes up), knowledge work (reading, writing, analyzing, deciding on a screen), and the executive thread that runs through both (choosing priorities, taking risks, owning what happens next). This profile is about the middle category — the keyboard half of the day — because that is where AI is most present and where the questions this assessment asks become answerable.

## AI Tools Currently in Use

AI has entered this person's work in the same ways it has entered most of the workforce. They have access to a general-purpose AI assistant — a tool they can open in a browser or an app and talk to directly. They encounter AI features baked into the software they already use: smart compose in email, summary generators in meeting tools, suggestions in spreadsheets and documents. They may or may not have an employer-issued AI policy. Either way, they are figuring out their own relationship to the tool in real time.

What they actually do with AI tends to fall into a few recognizable patterns: they use AI to find things out when a web search would be slower; they use it to think through something when they need a second brain; they use it to make a first draft of something they would rather not start from scratch; they use it to work through a document or a pile of data faster than reading it carefully would allow; and they use it to fit into the administrative and coordinative stream of a working week. The specifics change — the nurse manager is looking up drug interactions, the teacher is drafting parent emails, the owner-operator is comparing vendor quotes, the department supervisor is summarizing an incident — but the underlying uses are the same.

## Common Tasks

A typical week for this person includes a mix of tasks where AI is relevant and tasks where it isn't. The AI-relevant ones look something like this:

- Looking up information that needs to be accurate before it's acted on — a policy, a regulation, a procedure, a price, a precedent
- Thinking through a decision with competing considerations — which vendor, which approach, what to say, how to handle a situation
- Drafting something they'd rather not start from a blank page — an email, a memo, a summary, a short report, a set of notes
- Working through a document or a set of data that would otherwise take longer than available — a long email thread, a contract, a spreadsheet, meeting notes, a stack of updates
- Moving through the administrative layer of the week — scheduling, follow-ups, routine communications, the steady stream of things that need a response

Alongside these, the person spends significant time in the non-AI parts of their job: talking with people, handling issues as they come up, being present where presence matters, and making the calls that come with owning their piece of the work. The AI-adjacent tasks are a real part of the week, not the whole week.

## Decision Authority and Accountability

This person makes judgment calls throughout the day, but most operate within established patterns and expectations. They decide what to write, how to frame a communication, what to include in a summary, how to prioritize competing demands. They don't set organizational strategy, but they shape a lot of what the organization actually does.

**Routine decisions they own outright:** How to word an email. What to include in a routine report. How to organize their own notes. How to prioritize their own day. These are frequent, low-review decisions where the person's professional judgment is presumed and the output usually doesn't get double-checked before it lands. When AI helps here, the person is the sole quality gate.

**Judgment-embedded decisions that carry weight:** What to put in writing about a difficult situation. How to characterize a problem, a person, or a result. What to recommend when asked. Whether to flag a concern or handle it quietly. These decisions shape other people's actions and perceptions. An AI-assisted write-up that softens a real problem, or a recommendation shaped more by AI's pattern-matching than by the person's informed read of the situation, can send decisions in the wrong direction.

**Decisions they influence but don't own:** Bigger calls made by people above them — budget decisions, personnel decisions, strategic decisions, policy changes. The person provides the inputs: the summary, the recommendation, the analysis, the context. Their accountability is in the quality of those inputs. If AI introduces errors or distortions into what they provide, the downstream decision-maker has no way to know.

When this person gets it wrong, the consequences usually accumulate rather than explode. A single bad email doesn't end a career. But a pattern — unverified information in reports, AI-drafted communications that miss something important, summaries that quietly misrepresent what happened — erodes the trust others place in their work. That damage is slow and hard to trace, which is what makes it worth taking seriously.

## What's High-Stakes Here

The consequences of errors in this kind of work are real but varied:

- Information passed along to others — a colleague, a client, a customer, a parent, a patient, a supervisor — that turns out to be wrong or incomplete, where the person relaying it gets associated with the error
- Written records of difficult or consequential events — incident notes, performance documentation, status updates, client communications — that mischaracterize what actually happened
- Confidential information — personnel matters, financial data, private client or student or patient details, internal deliberations — appearing in places it shouldn't because the person pasted it into a tool without thinking about where it was going
- Recommendations or analyses that shape a decision someone above the person is going to make, where a quiet inaccuracy rides along into the decision
- The person's own professional credibility, which in most of these roles depends on being the person others can trust to know what they're talking about and to hand over work that holds up

Mistakes in this kind of work don't usually carry the immediate regulatory consequences of a specialist role. They carry reputational ones instead. A professional who sends along AI-drafted content with confident-sounding claims that don't hold up, or who shares sensitive information with tools that weren't meant to see it, erodes standing in ways that are hard to rebuild.

## Language and Terminology

The vocabulary of AI-adjacent work at this level is relatively cross-cutting. Terms that show up regularly: draft, first pass, cleanup, summary, source, verify, double-check, flag, pass it along, heads-up, handoff, context, background, paste in, run it through, check my work, make it shorter, make it sound better, the gist, the bottom line, what am I missing. These are the verbs and phrases of working with AI as a partner — asking it to do something, reviewing what it produced, deciding what to keep and what to redo.

The role-specific vocabulary varies by setting — a nurse manager's jargon differs from a teacher's differs from a small-business owner's — and scenarios in this assessment will specify their setting briefly rather than assume one shared workplace.

## What We Want to Know

When we assess someone in this kind of work, we want to understand: Can this person bring AI into their work in ways that genuinely help — finding things out faster, thinking through decisions more clearly, producing better first drafts, working through documents and data they'd otherwise skim, fitting AI into the administrative stream of the week — without sacrificing the accuracy, judgment, and appropriate care that their standing depends on? Do they know when AI's output is a useful starting point and when it needs real work before it gets passed along? Can they tell the difference between a task where AI should do most of the lifting and a task where AI should stay in a supporting role? Can they protect sensitive information while still getting the benefit of the tool? And can they hold on to their own professional judgment when an AI tool is presenting confident-sounding answers that may or may not be right?
